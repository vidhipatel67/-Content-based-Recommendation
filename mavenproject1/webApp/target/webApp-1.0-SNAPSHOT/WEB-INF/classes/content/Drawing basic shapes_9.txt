<p>To start off your drawing experience, consider drawing the most basic shape — a line. A canvas when viewed upon with regards to drawing routines can be expressed as an inverted <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" class="extiw" title="w:Cartesian coordinate system">Cartesian coordinate system</a>. A plane expressed by an x- and a y-axis. The origin point or <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="(0,0)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/c/1/5c16f757233856dcf311176b7410d2d5.png"> being the top-left corner of a canvas and the visible area of the canvas being the <b>Cartesian quadrant I</b> or the positive-positive <b>(+,+) quadrant</b>. The further you go down from the top, the greater the value of y-coordinate on the y-axis, vice-versa for the x-axis as you move toward the right from the left. And unlike the values on a normal graph, the values appear to be positive. So a point at <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="(10,20)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/8/f/38f1d3642abecbc03ceec527ac1db036.png"> would be 10 pixels away from the left and 20 pixels away from the top, hence the format <img class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline tex" alt="(x,y)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/0/c/90cbc22edf225adf8a68974f51227f05.png">.<br style="clear:both;"></p>
